preventing errors and creating clear options for return tickets

I worked with a UK national rail company to create clear options for return tickets and prevent a major error within their ticket purchase journey on web and app.

the brief

I was given a complaint received on Trustpilot revealing some issues with the user experience when booking a return ticket that combines peak and off peak times. I was asked to investigate all the possible problems when undertaking this customer journey, followed by suggestions on UX/UI improvements.

...when booking a return off peak on the website, it isn't immediately obvious what return trains are off peak and what aren't. You just get a schoolboy error message pop up. If you aren't familiar with the service, you are stuffed."
-Trustpilot review

understanding the problem

When does the error happen? I spent a day reading through every related complaint made in the past 3 months and watching any linked SessionCam videos. The budget did not have room for any usability testing so this research helped to give me a clear understanding of when the error was occuring. If someone chose an off-peak departure time and a return time that was during peak time the search results page would allow the customer to choose an off-peak return ticket but when they moved foward in the purchase journey they were given an error. Reading the complaints and watching the videos showed me just how frustrating this was for customers.

Why does the error happen? Understanding why this error was occurring required extensive conversations with the company's tech team and developers. It was a major problem with their backend system and how it pulled through results for return tickets. The research I did helped the team understand the extent and serious implications of the problem.

How often does it occur? In order for me to answer this question I needed to look at analytics specific to journeys in which this error message occured. Unfortunately the tags were not set up for this so I wasn't able to obtain the quantitative data I was after. I did advise the client to set this up immediately in case they had any pushback on development costs to implement the solution. My hypothesis was that the analytics would have shown high drop off rates for journeys in which this error occurred.

discovery workshop

bringing the problem to life

Rather than just describe the problem with screenshots, I started the workshop by showing the client actual footage from SessionCam of real customers struggling with the error and then trying to find information to recover from it. Then I read the Usabilla complaint that was connected to the footage. In doing this I shifted the focus away from "We are losing money." to "Wow. Our customers are really frustrated." This shift was extremely important. Rather than just improve the error message (the cheaper, easier option), the client was now on board with preventing the error altogether to create a truly better experience for their customers.

"I am trying to book an off-peak return. This shows me a number of trains, but when I click on any of them, I get a message saying the ticket I have chosen isn't valid for that time. Why in the name of all that is holy are you telling me I can travel on any of these trains with this ticket? Why have you made it so bloody difficult?"
-Frustrated customer

what are competitors doing?

I looked at several different competitors to see how they handled this issue. The solutions could be split into two options:

  • ✅ Allows customers to choose any time for departure or return.
  • ✅ Shows customers the best possible price.
  • ✅ Alerts customers if there is a better ticket available.

considering technical limitations

After looking at competitor solutions we discussed what was technically possbile for the client and what limitations they had in terms of capabilities and cost. I kept all of this in mind as I worked on design solutions.

viewing the solution through a customer's eyes

At this point, it would have been extremely beneficial to do usability testing on the client's purchase journey as well as Trainline and LNER. This would have helped me to understand any other pain points with the client's journey as well as any pain points with competitor solutions. Unfortunately this was not within the scope of this project, so in order to explore browsing activity and different scenarios in which off-peak tickets might be purchased, I created the persona of Lena.

meet lena...

  • Soon to be graduate of Manchester University
  • Currently in the recruitment process for a large tech company based in London
  • Needs to travel to London for an interview
  • On a strict budget and looking for the cheapest ticket
  • Knows that off-peak is usually cheapest but isn't sure when off-peak is

Lena helped me to think about all the different ways in which she might browse and use the search results page so that I could ensure the solution covered every eventuality.

exploring lena's behavior

Thinking about how Lena might use the search results page, I created 5 different scenarios and then designed a solution that worked for all of these.

bringing the solution to life

I designed a high fidelity prototype in Figma and created a prototype video with voiceover showing Lena's journey through each scenario.

The interaction design was complicated to explain in writing so the videos were an excellent supplement to the written documentation I provided for the design.

The videos were also used by the client in internal meetings to generate buy in for the project.

the outcome

With thorough research of the problem and careful consideration of technical limitations I was able to design a solution that supported inline ticket type changes and also alerted customers if a cheaper ticket was available. I was able to do this without making any major changes to the current search results page design saving time and cost for the development team. The solution also supported the backend updates required to solve the problem on the app resulting in a seamless journey across both channels.
